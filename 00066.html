readiness, appropriateness, fate

When someone is ready, they can act of their own volition. We can prepare somewhat, and that is what we call planning, learning. If the preparations turn out to be appropriate then we may in that case do well.

If we stop learning, or unable to deal with obstacles like illusions, diversions or deceptions, then our actions will be less appropriate.

Without this willingness to learn to act appropriately, people are forced to accept their fate, by definition, as they are not able to do anything about it.

We create our own fate, even though we sometimes don't remember this.

illusion of agency

am remarkably able to influence what I wish to learn and thus do. this may not be true, but is a nice illusion to hold

it is not a conspiracy, inevitability, or fate; but it is something that we can work to resolve when we choose to.

dealing with headstrong people who may have and will achieve great things but must be careful in later life that when they are no longer able to preserve the illusion of control that they do not fall apart.

perhaps the strongest and most insidious means of power and control is the control of someone's values.

agency

haters going to hate and lovers going to love, except in that one percent of times when change can happen. so both bad things and good things happening to you are mostly not because of what you have done or not done — yet there is still that one percent where you can change the world.

the course our life will run unless we are acted upon by external forces or we enact change upon ourselves. thus at once it is predetermined – in the sense of a flow set by our existing habits – but also malleable – in that what we do and face can change its course.

People can seem accepting of everything when actually they believe in two very disparate beliefs.

  • One is belief in surrender. They believe that change is impossible, that all is beyond their control. This is what abusers want their victims to believe.
  • The other is belief in agency. We believe we can change, that often it easier that we think, that what we do makes a difference.
  • The two beliefs themselves are not so very different — both are about what we believe about change. Yet believing in one leads to death, decay and destruction. And believing in the other leads to life, growth and creation.

self–determination

  • “one can lead a horse to water, but one cannot force him to drink”.
  • as i like to extend the proverb (the horse) chooses himself whether to drink.
  • what people forget is there is more than one tendency. our genes, epigenes, environment, culture; none of these is monolithic. this is why our fate is undecided until we decide it ourselves.

a simple task uncovers a postdictive illusion of choice

adam bear and paul bloom 2016
http://doi.org/10.1177/0956797616641943
Link: 0956797616641943

  • Do people know when, or whether, they have made a conscious choice? Here, we explore the possibility that choices can seem to occur before they are actually made. In two studies, participants were asked to quickly choose from a set of options before a randomly selected option was made salient. Even when they believed that they had made their decision prior to this event, participants were significantly more likely than chance to report choosing the salient option when this option was made salient soon after the perceived time of choice. Thus, without participants’ awareness, a seemingly later event influenced choices that were experienced as occurring at an earlier time. These findings suggest that, like certain low-level perceptual experiences, the experience of choice is susceptible to “postdictive” influence and that people may systematically overestimate the role that consciousness plays in their chosen behavior.
