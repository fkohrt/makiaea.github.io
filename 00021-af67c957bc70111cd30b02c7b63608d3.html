  • “i know things they don’t know!” the role of need for uniqueness in belief in conspiracy theories
    anthony lantian et al. 2017
    https://doi.org/10.1027/1864-9335/a000306

    • In the current research, we investigated whether belief in conspiracy theories satisfies people’s need for uniqueness. We found that the tendency to believe in conspiracy theories was associated with the feeling of possessing scarce information about the situations explained by the conspiracy theories (Study 1) and higher need for uniqueness (Study 2). Further two studies using two different manipulations of need for uniqueness (Studies 3 and 4) showed that people in a high need for uniqueness condition displayed higher conspiracy belief than people in a low need for uniqueness condition. This conclusion is strengthened by a small-scale meta-analysis. These studies suggest that conspiracy theories may serve people’s desire to be unique, highlighting a motivational underpinning of conspiracy belief.

  • too special to be duped: need for uniqueness motivates conspiracy beliefs
    roland imhoff, pia karoline lamberty 2017
    https://doi.org/10.1002/ejsp.2265

    • Adding to the growing literature on the antecedents of conspiracy beliefs, this paper argues that a small part in motivating the endorsement of such seemingly irrational beliefs is the desire to stick out from the crowd, the need for uniqueness. Across three studies, we establish a modest but robust association between the self‐attributed need for uniqueness and a general conspirational mindset (conspiracy mentality) as well as the endorsement of specific conspiracy beliefs. Following up on previous findings that people high in need for uniqueness resist majority and yield to minority influence, Study 3 experimentally shows that a fictitious conspiracy theory received more support by people high in conspiracy mentality when this theory was said to be supported by only a minority (vs. majority) of survey respondents. Together, these findings support the notion that conspiracy beliefs can be adopted as a means to attain a sense of uniqueness.

    • cognitive luck
      • “One of the reasons why the notion of bounded rationality is challenged, and more idealized notions of rationality are advocated, is to avoid the phenomenon of cognitive luck, according to which decisions are rational only because the strategy used to arrive at the decision happens to match the structure of the environment in which the decision is made (Matheson 2006). If rationality is a matter of luck, then the prospect for improving our cognitive performance is grim. The challenge for ecological rationality and other notions of bounded rationality is to preserve the normative dimension of rationality. These notions may be really useful to describe what human agents do when they reason in certain environments, but are they also able to provide a framework for the evaluation and enhancement of human reasoning? In other words, is there room for irrationality in the picture of decision-making that Gigerenzer and his colleagues sketch?”

    • feyerabend
      • “Paul Feyerabend is one of the most influential authors to argue that anti-rationalism about science constitutes a challenge for the hegemony of science in contemporary society. In a collection of lectures recently made available under the title of The Tyranny of Science (2011), Feyerabend argues for the importance of promoting a variety of human endeavours and interests (i.e., religion, the arts, philosophy and science) and of embracing conflict among them as opposed to attempting to achieve perfect harmony. Conflict is, according to Feyerabend, the key to scientific progress. In a classic work, entitled Against Method, originally published in the 1970s, Feyerabend endorses the proliferation of different methodologies. His slogan is: ‘Anything goes’. Great discoveries are not made by following rigid methodological prescriptions, but are precipitated by random events.”
      • “[T]he idea of a fixed method, or of a fixed theory of rationality, rests on too naive a view of man and his social surroundings. To those who look at the rich material provided by history, and who are not intent on impoverishing it in order to please their lower instincts, their craving for intellectual security in the form of clarity, precision, ‘objectivity’, ‘truth’, it will become clear that there is only one principle that can be defended under all circumstances and in all stages of human development. It is the principle: anything goes. (Feyerabend 1975: 18–19)
      • Through the recognition of different ways of thinking, Feyerabend celebrates the openness of mind necessary for embracing new ideas. Successful scientific theories are not based on facts, but on interpretations of facts, and divergences of opinion survive in scientific communities, stimulating further research and resulting in the formation of novel hypotheses to explain long-standing problems. Although other philosophers and sociologists of science have argued against the apparent objectivity of science, and against the alleged superiority of the scientific way of thinking, what makes Feyerabend's work significant in a discourse on the meaning and scope of human rationality is that he was genuinely interested in challenging the identification of the irrational with the unscientific.”
    • “Self-narratives can fail to tell true stories about the self, but what is interesting about them is that they have the power to influence or determine an agent’s present and future behaviour. That is why a very effective way for an agent to change behaviour and abandon maladaptive ways of thinking or bad habits is to edit her personal narrative. What Tim Wilson (2013) calls story editing is ‘a set of techniques to redirect people’s narratives about themselves and the social world in a way that leads to lasting changes in behavior’ (p. 155).”

  • taboos are like uncharted reefs in our lives

  • denial

    • when people feel they need to defend their sense of self, they will not be able to grow. denial of reality is perhaps the worst manifestation of this
    • you might as well give up completely on someone who is self–righteous. because their ability to question their way of life is no longer available, all they will hear from you is criticism, since they are so far from reality that there is little with which you can agree with them, and even then their interpretation is negative. sadly, if your spouse, family or best friend is self–righteous, although you do not want to leave them, eventually to preserve your life you will need to. it is so difficult to weather the unceasing burden that the self–righteous impose on us.
    • like visual blind spots, the beliefs that control us make holes in our perception of reality — we literally do not sense or interpret many parts of what is there — and also make illusions from what we take in
    • worst atrocities perpetuated by those who believe themselves pure. this is not rejecting purity, rather it is acknowledging truth. this is the mechanism by which we deny the consequences of our actions.
      • to deal with this denial, look at the results of your actions, not what you feel about what you have done.
      • just because the results of our actions does not fit our images of ourselves as innocent pure beings, is no justification to deny their reality. we need to deal with reality and address this denial.
      • it is often counter–productive to criticize someone who is in denial, as they would have done better if they could. just get on with your life and try to minimize the effect their actions have on your life if you can.
      • the unfortunate reality is that almost all people in the world are in denial
      • pay attention to what they do, don't get too enamoured with what they say. if they say one thing but do another…
    • saying no is as important as saying yes, but when someone only knows how to say no, they will lose many opportunities in life.
      • religious fundamentalism is only one of the most obvious ways of saying no.
      • refusing to consider different views or denying they have any validity or misunderstanding what someone means without trying to really understand it first — these are more subtle ways of saying no but they are still ways of saying no nonetheless
    • vested interest is not criticism of individual motivations but tendency for aggregate to behave in a self–interested manner

  • delusion

    • delusions, and their product, abuse, are ways of avoiding certain aspects of life. what are we avoiding? the injunction, “deal with it”, is to face what we are avoiding, to change the delusion and abuse to something more benign.
    • reading someone’s work is like finding a partial key to their delusions — i wonder what you can find in our writing, that we do not know?
