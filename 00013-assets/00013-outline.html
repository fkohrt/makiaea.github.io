(this is the assumption we wish to question) the only solution given that output of sensitive is to match that of insensitive (equality – also assumed) then the only way for this to be achieved is for the sensitive to drastically reduce their ability to capture sensation –– to die a partial death, in other words.

      • if someone is closed minded, it can often be pointless to try to convince them of reality. if you were one of them, would you be reading this? but there are reasons and causes of closed mindedness and it would be unlikely that anyone is always open minded.
      • not only can tell when a person is lying, but also full empathy allows us to really understand and cherish people. many people when they first meet us already get some idea that we have this ability, but few take the time to understand what it actually means, as we use the ability to understand people, rather than to get people to do what we want because we have made them aware of what our ability is.
        • not only understand what kind of person someone is and wants to be but also what they can possibly be, when they are understood by someone. because we do not expect them to behave as they have done or are expected to behave, they have the opportunity to behave differently, and in most cases, better than they would have when they could not fully trust the person in front of them.
      • judgement–free understanding completely how things really are, not only how they can be or how we wish them to be.
      • hearing sounds and recognising meaning in them can be two different things. my hearing isn't especially acute, nor is my understanding. but used in combination with an openness to understanding, it can all become a seemingly amazing ability.
        • I've always wanted to not be labelled a genius and this is one way of helping people understand that what we sometimes label as genius is simply unhampered or less hampered natural human ability.
        • it's like being able to recognise what someone is saying, when the sounds are muffled by earphones, or recognising the meaning of messy handwriting — we know that something is there, the sound or the handwriting, and also recognise what it means. so too with what we say or do — the deeper meaning of our words and actions may seem far from what we say or do
      • being able to learn new things as well as learning what is beyond those new things
      • I like to think of myself as open, but in fact it is just that relative to my peers I have a relatively open set point — if we were comparing to people a hundred years from now, I might not seem so open. for example, I don't do drugs (including most intoxicants like alcohol, or even tea, coffee or most processed sugar — all of these are accepted my almost all of my peers)

        the point is, I do have a set point, a set of habits that seem non–negotiable, as almost all people do (I haven't met a person that disproves this theory, but they may exist somewhere).

    • numbing effects
      • from painkiller to empathy killer: acetaminophen (paracetamol) reduces empathy for pain
        dominik mischkowski, jennifer crocker and baldwin m. way 2016
        doi.org/10.1093/scan/nsw057
        Link:doi.org/10.1093/scan/nsw057
        • “Simulation theories of empathy hypothesize that empathizing with others’ pain shares some common psychological computations with the processing of one’s own pain. Support for this perspective has largely relied on functional neuroimaging evidence of an overlap between activations during the experience of physical pain and empathy for other people’s pain. Here, we extend the functional overlap perspective to the neurochemical level and test whether a common physical painkiller, acetaminophen (paracetamol), can reduce empathy for another’s pain. In two double-blind placebo-controlled experiments, participants rated perceived pain, personal distress and empathic concern in response to reading scenarios about another’s physical or social pain, witnessing ostracism in the lab, or visualizing another study participant receiving painful noise blasts. As hypothesized, acetaminophen reduced empathy in response to others’ pain. Acetaminophen also reduced the unpleasantness of noise blasts delivered to the participant, which mediated acetaminophen’s effects on empathy. Together, these findings suggest that the physical painkiller acetaminophen reduces empathy for pain and provide a new perspective on the neurochemical bases of empathy. Because empathy regulates prosocial and antisocial behavior, these drug-induced reductions in empathy raise concerns about the broader social side effects of acetaminophen, which is taken by almost a quarter of adults in the United States each week.”
        • “A variant of this approach was recently used to show that placebo analgesia reduces empathy for others, an effect mediated by the opioid system (Rütgen et al., 2015a,b).”
        • “In summary, our research is the first to show that the popular physical painkiller acetaminophen can reduce empathy to the pain of others. Physical pain is an aversive experience, and un- doubtedly many people can attest to acetaminophen’s benefi- cial ability to suppress physical pain. However, acetaminophen can also have unappreciated psychosocial side effects by inter- rupting the fundamental capacity to empathically connect with other people’s painful experiences. Quite literally, acetamino- phen reduces one’s ability to feel another’s pain.”
