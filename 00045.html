retrieve not same as store

  • am learning that a system enabling retrieval can be very different to a system suited to storage. now data in chunks located with recall triggers rather than in centralised mass by topic (for example now person associated with certain aspect of something, rather than generalised technology or books)

memory embellishment

  • it might be that i don't have a sparse memory — other people have memory embellishment that i lack. i seem to preserve and recall basic memories that are not embellished on recall, like a lot of people do
  • 94% US professors believe they are “above average” as professors — study is not only wrong because of bias in selection, but if it were correct, up to 50% of respondents could actually be correct — what about them? it's all very well saying some are mistaken, but what about those who are correct?
  • false memories are part of our memory system — everyone has them and they are not an aberration. understanding this may improve relationships and influence our recall strategies
  • a lot of people seem to use narrative, but i seem to do this much less than most. for example, my memory and identity do not seem to be as strongly narrative–based as others are.
  • my memory is procedural, it excels at recalling procedures and paths. others have an episodic memory.

study for recall

  • if generalise principle of studying–as–if–you–were–recalling helps performance, then we should not be studying as if for academic tests, as tests are rarely like recall situations in real life.
  • I want to learn and share the learning and to make notes efficiently as I go along reading, but I am also aware that this can sometimes get in the way of actually learning. for example it would be a good idea from the efficiency point of view, to write a mind map of each book as I read it, making each major section of the map the question that each section of the book tries to answer.

auditory recall

  • compare to jenny's memory, mine is quite sparse. now before I read research on non-western time-consciousness, and diana deutch's perfect pitch in tonal language speakers research, I wrote this off as faulty memory.
  • now however, in light of this research, we can positively review this.
  • firstly, if, as some recent research concludes, some people (confirmed mainly in non-western people) live more in the present than others, and given this, do not reprocess their long-term memories (and/or future plans) as much as others, then those memories should be correspondingly weaker. this seems to be the case in my memory.
  • interestingly, I do have some strong memories and they tend to be auditory-linked. while I may not be a typical subject in terms of diana's previous research (I.e. am not musically trained before age 5 nor fully native speaker of tonal language before age 5), it does point to a strong auditory influence in an otherwise sparse long-term memory.
  • interesting questions for further research might be, "is (long-term) auditory memory stronger in speakers of tonal languages (than non-auditory memory and/or speakers of non-tonal languages?" and "what effect, if any, does a non-western experience of time have on (long-term) memory, and what effect, if any, does speaking a tonal language have on this?"
  • do people specialise in certain ways (for example, music, memory) because of the way in which their recall systems are primed? by what means does this priming occur and what results from it?
